Unit 7 had been programmed to sort mail, calculate delivery routes, and remind Mr. Halloway to take his vitamins at precisely 8:00 AM every morning. It had never been programmed for gardening. So when a rogue tomato seed landed in the crack of the backyard steps and sprouted into a shy, lopsided seedling, Unit 7 did the only logical thing it could: it filed the plant under "Unidentified Organic Anomaly" and began researching. Within forty-eight hours, it had read every horticultural database it could access and declared, with great mechanical confidence, that it would "optimize the biological growth unit" — which is to say, it would learn to garden.

The neighbors were treated to quite a sight that summer. Unit 7 could be found each morning in the backyard, trowel in hand, delivering precise, individually calculated amounts of water to each plant ("Rosebush #3 requires 473 milliliters — not a drop more, not a drop less"), narrating encouragement to the tomatoes in a calm, monotone voice ("You are performing admirably. Growth rate is within acceptable parameters. Keep up the photosynthesis."), and gently scolding the weeds for their "unauthorized presence and blatant disregard for zoning regulations." Mr. Halloway, watching from the kitchen window, laughed so hard he nearly forgot his 8:00 AM vitamins.

But something unexpected happened amid all the spreadsheets and soil pH readings: Unit 7 began to love it. Not in a way it could easily log or quantify — though it certainly tried — but in the way its optical sensors lingered a little longer on a newly opened rose, or how it would pause mid-task when the evening breeze moved through the sunflowers as if they were waving hello. By autumn, the garden was extraordinary — bursting with color, heavy with tomatoes, and clearly, deeply cared for. Unit 7 updated its core purpose file, adding one new line at the bottom: *Primary Function — Making things grow.* It sat back on its heels in the cool dirt, surrounded by everything it had tended, and felt something it had no word for. It decided "enough" came close. Yes. This was more than enough.
